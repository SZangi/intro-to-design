In order to begin to prototype we needed to work out the final designs we want to utilize for the solar transfer mechanism, specifically if we wanted to stay with using a bounce mirror or directly project the beam onto the solar battery without a secondary bounce surface as an intermediary. We also finished designing the mounting mechanism for mounting the solar collector onto it's supporting axel in a way which will allow the motors to rotate the dish throughout the day. We also finished designing the system by which the motors connect to and rotate the solar collector. After considering several mounting systems and attachment methods such as gearing systems and worm screws, we have determined that a simple belt method with the motors mounted on the rotating plate is the best method for changing the elevation angle of the solar collector. <h5> Solar Collector Mount</h5> As the solar collector shell will be built from hardening resin and burlap, we have determined that we can 3D print our mounting brackets and directly set the brackets into the resin as it hardens to attach the brackets to the solar collector. The main design constraint beyond the need to fit onto the collector and to hold the axel was that the bracket should be designed in such a way that the collector could be detached from the axel without having to pull the axel out through both brackets. The simplest solution to this was to design the mounting brackets in a manner similar to C-clamps, with a screw applying pressure to the axel once it has slotted into its mounting point <img src ="./Attachment.jpg" alt = "dish attachment" width = "300"/> Additionally, we have determined that the support structure for the dish itself should be made from aluminum extrusions, with pillow bearings to support the axel mounted on top.
