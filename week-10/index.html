In order to begin to prototype we needed to work out the final designs we want to utilize for the solar transfer mechanism, specifically if we wanted to stay with using a bounce mirror or directly project the beam onto the solar battery without a secondary bounce surface as an intermediary. We also finished designing the mounting mechanism for mounting the solar collector onto it's supporting axel in a way which will allow the motors to rotate the dish throughout the day. We also finished designing the system by which the motors connect to and rotate the solar collector. After considering several mounting systems and attachment methods such as gearing systems and worm screws, we have determined that a simple belt method with the motors mounted on the rotating plate is the best method for changing the elevation angle of the solar collector. <h5> Solar Collector Mount</h5> As the solar collector shell will be built from hardening resin and burlap, we have determined that we can 3D print our mounting brackets and directly set the brackets into the resin as it hardens to attach the brackets to the solar collector. The main design constraint beyond the need to fit onto the collector and to hold the axel was that the bracket should be designed in such a way that the collector could be detached from the axel without having to pull the axel out through both brackets. The simplest solution to this was to design the mounting brackets in a manner similar to C-clamps, with a screw applying pressure to the axel once it has slotted into its mounting point, and both keeping the axel attached to the collector and preventing the axel from rotating in the bracket. By forcing the axel to be static relative to the collector, we have also met the design constraint that the solar collector needs to rotate with our axel so that our solar tracking system will be able to function. A 3D model of the bracket is shown below. <img src ="./bracket.jpg" alt = "mounting bracket" width = "300"/> Additionally, we have determined that the axel bearings on the support posts should be ball bearings mounted in angle brackets, as opposed to pillow bearings as originally intended. This is due to the price of pillow bearings relative to most ball bearings as well as the relative availability of the standard ball bearings. A picture of our current full assembly is shown below. <img src ="./AzimuthalAssembly.jpg" alt = "assembly" width = "300"/> <h4> Prototyping</h4> This week we also began prototyping important parts of our design in the beaverworks shop. We began by laser cutting the gears we will need to rotate the base of the solar collector from various widths of delrin, a plastic polymer, to determine the laser cutter's efficacy in fabricating the parts we need. From this we have determined that our gears will best be constructed of two laminated gears of 0.2" delrin, as opposed to one piece of 0.375" delrin as we initially planned. We also began laser cutting the parts necessary to construct the plywood base and motor housing which the solar collector and molten salt battery assemblies rest on.
